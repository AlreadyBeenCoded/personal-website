Background · texture study

Five ways to warm the page.

Each option shown against your bone base with sage, clay and stone. They're tuned to a realistic ship-level subtlety — quiet on purpose. Scroll to compare, and note how the last one behaves differently when you're not touching it.

01

Static grain

A faint monochrome noise layer. Kills the flat-digital-white feeling and reads as paper. The most on-brand, most invisible option.

How — inline SVG feTurbulence, ~10% opacity, multiply blend. No image files, negligible weight.
02

Washi paper

Directional fibres plus a fine speckle, on a warmer bone. A quiet Japanese nod with a touch more character than pure noise.

How — approximated here with SVG; on the real site, swap in one optimised paper texture for true fibre detail.
03

Faint mesh · time-of-day

A barely-there wash — sage in one corner, clay in another, stone through the middle. Drifts so slowly it feels like ambient light, not an effect. This is where your day-to-dusk palette shift lives.

How — pure CSS radial gradients + a 40s drift. No JS. Reduced-motion pins it still.
04

Drifting grain

The same noise as 01, shimmering film-grain style. Adds life without motion you'd consciously track. The most it should ever move.

How — stepped background-position animation. Must be gated behind reduced-motion.
05

Cursor-reactive glow

A soft sage light that follows the pointer. Move your cursor across this panel. This is the "interactive background" tier — and the first thing that can tip minimalism into template territory.

How — JS updates two CSS vars; a radial gradient tracks them. Fades to still on touch devices.
Recommendation

My honest pick: 01 or 02 as the base, plus 03 for the time-of-day shift — three layers that all whisper. I'd keep 04 for the hero only, and hold 05 in reserve rather than running it site-wide. Every option here already respects prefers-reduced-motion.
